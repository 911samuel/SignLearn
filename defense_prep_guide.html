1. The field at a glance

Sign-language recognition has moved through three clear eras. SignLearn sits at the leading edge of the third, with one foot stepping into the fourth.

In one sentence (for non-technical panellists)
Scientists have been trying to teach computers to read sign language for thirty years. The methods have improved enormously, but no system has yet combined high accuracy, low cost, and a two-way conversation in one product. SignLearn does.

The three eras of sign-language recognition

1990s – early 2000s

Era 1 — Sensor gloves

Researchers wired up gloves with bend-sensors and gyroscopes (Starner, Pentland, others). Accuracy was high, but users had to wear ugly hardware. Unusable in real life.

2000s – mid-2010s

Era 2 — Classical computer vision

Cameras replaced gloves. Hand-crafted features (HOG, SIFT) and traditional classifiers (SVM, HMM) read the hand from raw video. Worked in the lab; failed when lighting, background, or signer changed.

2015 – today

Era 3 — Deep learning

CNNs and recurrent networks (LSTM, GRU) learned features directly from video. WLASL (2020) and ASL-Citizen (2024) made benchmark data available. Accuracy crossed the 90 % threshold for isolated signs — but GPU requirements kept these systems out of everyday hands. This is where SignLearn lives.

2022 – emerging

Era 4 — Continuous signing & transformers

SignBERT, MS-G3D, and CTC-trained transformers attack the harder problem of reading full sentences with no manual segmentation. The frontier — and where we point the project next.

The three problems no one has solved together

Gap 1

One-way only

Almost every published system converts sign → text or speech → text — not both in one session, with one shared transcript.

Gap 2

Hardware-bound

State-of-the-art systems demand GPUs, depth cameras (Kinect, LeapMotion), or sensor gloves. They never deploy outside research labs.

Gap 3

Lab-only accuracy

Published benchmarks collapse under real lighting, real signers, and real backgrounds. The 95 % number in a paper rarely survives a webcam.

3. What SignLearn adds to the existing research

The building blocks are all known. The contribution is the combination that no published system offers together.

"MediaPipe + sequence model" is not new. This specific combination — empirically chosen, CPU-deployable, bidirectional, fully open — is.
Contribution 1

Empirical model selection

Instead of picking one architecture and reporting only its accuracy, we trained six architectures (LSTM, BiLSTM, Transformer, CNN-BiLSTM, Conformer-Lite, TCN) under identical conditions — same splits, seed, epochs, batch — and published the per-run reports for all twelve sweep configurations.

Why it matters: The 97.84 % headline is supported by a transparent comparison, not a private hyperparameter search. Five of the six architectures crossed 96 %, which is itself a finding: landmark normalisation matters more than the choice of temporal backbone.

Contribution 2

Sub-millisecond CPU inference

By exporting the TCN to ONNX Runtime, the production model runs at p95 0.23 ms per sample (~4,890 fps) on a single CPU core — about 14× faster than the same model in Keras and well below the 30 ms real-time budget.

Why it matters: Removes the GPU requirement that gates almost all prior real-time SLR work. Every laptop in this room can run it. Every classroom and clinic computer can run it.

Contribution 3

Genuine bidirectional communication

One WebSocket session carries the signer's sign-to-text channel and the hearing user's speech-to-text channel into one shared transcript. Turn-taking is natural; neither user has to translate.

Why it matters: Removes the asymmetric translation burden that today forces Deaf users to carry the conversation. Most prior systems are sign-to-text only.

Contribution 4

Full open-artefact release

Every per-run report, the dataset audit, the ONNX checkpoint, the training scripts, the latency profile, and the thesis itself are public. The headline result reproduces from a clean clone in six make commands.

Why it matters: A reviewer can verify every number in the thesis without trusting us. The work earns the Verifiability pillar by construction, not by claim.

Two findings that contribute to the literature

Finding A

Data > architecture, for landmark SLR

Five of six architectures crossed 96 % under identical conditions. This is not consistently reported in the landmark-based SLR literature, and it implies the next wave of progress will come from better data — not better model designs.

Finding B

The WLASL → ASL-Citizen lift

The same model family on a comparably sized vocabulary went from 26.0 % top-1 (WLASL) to 87.4 % top-1 (ASL-Citizen) — a 3.4× improvement driven by signer diversity and capture quality, not architecture. This is the single largest intervention in the project.

4. Why we chose each piece

Every important decision in the system has a defensible reason, anchored in measured numbers from our own runs.

Why MediaPipe Hands (and not a CNN that reads pixels)

For non-technical readers
A normal sign-language system tries to read the whole video frame — every pixel — to figure out what the hand is doing. We do something smarter: we let MediaPipe find just the 42 important points on the hands first (21 per hand), and then we only look at those points. It's like reading a connect-the-dots picture instead of staring at a photograph.

Technical reasons:

  • 30 fps in the browser, on any laptop. No GPU required.
  • Privacy by default. Raw video never leaves the browser — only the 126-dimensional landmark vector crosses the network.
  • Signer-invariant by construction. Wrist-centring + unit-scaling means absolute position and camera distance drop out.
  • 1000× smaller input. A 480×640 RGB frame is ~921,600 numbers; the landmark vector is 126.

Why TCN (and not LSTM, Transformer, or anything else)

For non-technical readers
Think of TCN as a "sliding window" approach that looks at the past second of motion in parallel. LSTMs read the same data one frame at a time, which is slower. Transformers are more flexible but cost more memory and run slower on CPUs. We tested all six and picked the one that won on the trade-off between accuracy, model size, and speed.
ArchitectureTest accVerdict
LSTM96.4 %Strong, but slower training and a larger ONNX export.
BiLSTM96.7 %Same family, marginal gain.
Transformer96.9 %Competitive, but multi-head attention is slower under ONNX-on-CPU.
CNN-BiLSTM97.2 %Best non-winner. Larger parameter count.
Conformer-Lite96.5 %Most complex, no accuracy reward.
TCN97.84 %Won. Highest accuracy, only 126,372 parameters, fastest ONNX path.

Why ONNX (and not Keras for serving)

Same model, two runtimes:

2.88 msKeras p50 0.23 msONNX p95 14×speedup 538 KBONNX file

ONNX removes the Python tensor-framework runtime, fuses graph operations at load time, and runs hand-tuned CPU kernels. Lower latency means a slower laptop can still run the system, which means more potential users.

Why WebSocket (and not REST)

REST would force the client to poll for every prediction — wasteful at 30 fps. WebSocket holds one TCP connection per signer and streams frames in, predictions out. End-to-end round-trip: 40.66 ms mean, 127 ms p95.

Why Next.js (and not vanilla React or Vite)

Three reasons: (a) App Router + server components match the future deployment story (Vercel edge + Render backend); (b) TypeScript-first ergonomics; (c) the MediaPipe Tasks API integrates cleanly with the Next.js client component lifecycle.

Why wrist-centred unit-scaling normalisation

This is the single piece of code that does the most recognition work. Centring on the wrist makes the absolute position of the signer in the frame irrelevant. Unit-scaling by maximum inter-landmark distance makes the camera distance irrelevant. The result: the same sign at 30 cm and at 1 m produces nearly identical input tensors. Validated empirically — five of six architectures cross 96 % on the normalised features.

Why a 30-frame buffer (and not a different window)

At 30 fps, 30 frames is one second — the natural duration of a static fingerspelling pose. Shorter windows miss motion context; longer windows add latency and overfit static signs. The thesis Future Work names a learned-window CTC architecture as the next step.

Why the EMA smoother with 0.75 threshold and 15-frame cooldown

Raw softmax outputs at 30 fps are too noisy to surface directly. EMA dampens single-frame jitter; the 0.75 confidence gate rejects low-confidence emissions before they reach the UI; the 15-frame (500 ms) cooldown prevents the same letter from being re-emitted on every prediction tick. These three constants were tuned on a held-out webcam recording session and never adjusted against the test set — so they do not contaminate the 97.84 % number.

5. How it works end-to-end

Eight steps from a hand in front of a webcam to a word in a transcript.

#WhereWhat happens
1BrowserThe webcam captures a frame at ~30 fps.
2BrowserMediaPipe Hands locally extracts 21 landmarks per hand → 126 floats.
3Browser → ServerThe 126-float vector is emitted over WebSocket. Raw video never leaves the device.
4ServerFrameBuffer normalises each frame (wrist-centred, unit-scaled) and pushes it onto a 30-frame circular buffer.
5ServerWhen the buffer fills, the (30, 126) tensor is passed to the ONNX-Runtime TCN, which produces a 36-way softmax in 0.23 ms.
6ServerThe PredictionSmoother applies EMA, the 0.75 confidence gate, and the 15-frame cooldown. About 14 % of frames are suppressed.
7Server → BrowserIf the smoother approves, a {label, confidence} message is emitted back over WebSocket.
8BrowserThe predicted letter is appended to the shared transcript visible to both the signer and the hearing user.

The hearing user's path is symmetric: microphone → Web Speech API (in-browser) → transcribed text → same shared transcript. The two conversation streams write to one log; turn-taking is natural.

6. Tough questions, direct answers

Every question below was asked or anticipated during preparation. Click any question to expand. Each answer is structured for both technical and non-technical panellists.

Technical methodology

Why TCN over Transformer when Transformer also crossed 96 %? Technical
Technical answer

Three numerical reasons. (1) TCN reached 97.84 % vs Transformer's 96.9 %; on 11,417 test samples that's ~100 fewer errors. (2) TCN has 126,372 parameters; the Transformer is larger, which matters for our ONNX-on-CPU constraint. (3) Dilated convolutions are ONNX-Runtime friendly; multi-head attention is slower on CPU. We chose on the accuracy × parameters × latency frontier, not on accuracy alone.

For a non-technical panellist

Both architectures are good. TCN is slightly more accurate, smaller, and faster on the kind of computer most people own. We picked the one that wins on all three.

Is 97.84 % real, or are you overfitting? Technical
Technical answer

Three checks. (1) Validation-to-test gap is ~0.3 pp. (2) Macro precision 96.25 %, macro recall 95.32 %, macro F1 95.45 % — all within 2.5 pp of the headline, so it isn't propped up by one dominant class. (3) The split is signer-aware: no signer appears in both train and test (artifacts/reports/audit_signers.md). Overfitting would show as a class with very high recall and near-zero precision; the per-class report doesn't contain one.

For a non-technical panellist

We never let the model see the same person's hand in training and testing. The number isn't memorisation — it's recognition.

You used seed=42 for everything. How do you know this isn't lucky? Technical
Technical answer

It is the principal threat to the headline and we name it directly in §5.7. Two mitigations: five of twelve sweep configurations crossed 96 %, which is unlikely under a single lucky initialisation; and the macro precision/recall/F1 sit within 2.5 pp of the headline. A full cross-seed bootstrap is the first item in §6.4.

For a non-technical panellist

Yes — we acknowledge this honestly. We didn't try a hundred random seeds and pick the best one; we set one number and ran everything against it. The number we picked happened to work, but five different model designs all crossed the same high bar with the same setting — so the result isn't an accident.

How is macro F1 95.45 % compatible with 47.6 % recall on the digit "two"? Technical
Technical answer

Both are real. The ASL handshape for the digit "two" is identical to the letter "V" — extended index and middle finger. Same for "six" and "W", and "zero" and "O". The model isn't failing — it's correctly classifying a hand that is, in ASL, ambiguous between two labels. ~1 % of total test error is linguistic, not a model bug, and the thesis quantifies this in §5.3.2.

For a non-technical panellist

The number "two" and the letter "V" are signed with the exact same hand shape. There is no way for any model — or any human, without context — to tell them apart from the hand alone. We report this honestly rather than hiding it.

Why no continuous signing? Isn't that the real problem? Technical
Technical answer

Continuous signing requires CTC-loss training on sentence-level segmented video and a different smoother — substantially more data and a different research problem. The landmark feature pipeline we built is directly reusable; only the loss and inference smoother change. Named in §6.4 as a 12-month item, with the recording infrastructure already in place.

For a non-technical panellist

That's the next chapter. We deliberately scoped this project to the part we could do well, and we built the foundation that the next chapter needs.

Comparisons with existing systems

Google has MediaPipe ASL demos. Aren't you reinventing it? Comparison
Technical answer

We use the MediaPipe team's landmark extraction — that contribution is theirs. Everything downstream is ours: the empirical six-architecture sweep with open per-run reports, the ONNX export pathway, the bidirectional WebSocket transcript, and the open release. None of these are in the MediaPipe demos.

For a non-technical panellist

Google makes the tool we use to see the hand. We built the system that decides what the hand means — and the conversation that wraps around it. Google has not shipped that.

The WLASL paper reports 60–75 % top-1 on similar data. You report 87.4 %. Why is yours so much higher? Comparison
Technical answer

Because we are not comparing on the same data. The 87.4 % is on ASL-Citizen, a higher-quality dataset (~3× more signers per class, controlled capture, no YouTube domain shift). On WLASL itself, our same architecture reaches 26.0 % top-1 — squarely in the range the literature reports. The 3.4× lift is a data-quality result, not a model-quality result. We document the pivot transparently in §3.1.2 and §5.4.

For a non-technical panellist

Better data, not a smarter model. We changed where we trained from, not how we train.

SignSpeak and other commercial ASL apps already exist — what is new here? Comparison
Technical answer

SignSpeak is a respected commercial system. Where we differ: (1) we publish per-run reports for every architecture, not just the winner; (2) we run on CPU at p95 0.23 ms — SignSpeak requires a GPU; (3) we ship both directions through one shared transcript; (4) we are fully open-source. The contribution is not "better than SignSpeak" — it's "verifiable and deployable on commodity hardware, in combination."

For a non-technical panellist

They make a product. We made a system that anyone — including future researchers — can inspect, copy, and improve. Different goal, same underlying field.

Ethics & privacy

You're streaming a Deaf person's body movements to a server. What is the privacy story? Ethics
Technical answer

Raw video never leaves the browser. MediaPipe runs locally; only the 126-float landmark vector crosses the WebSocket. Audio is identical — Web Speech API runs in-browser, only transcribed text crosses the wire. An intercepted stream reveals hand position, not face, environment, or identity. Source: backend/api/socket_handlers.py.

For a non-technical panellist

The webcam stays on your computer. We never see your face, your room, or your voice — only the 21 points on each hand, as numbers. Even if someone intercepted those numbers, they would not be able to identify you.

Did Deaf people consent to being in your training data? Ethics
Technical answer

The Kaggle ASL datasets carry their published licences and signer demographics are not documented; many are inferred to be hearing ASL learners. ASL-Citizen — the source for the word-level extension — was collected by Microsoft Research with documented Deaf-community participation protocols. We did not collect any new training data ourselves, so direct consent collection by our team was not required. The community-recording extension named in §6.4 would require formal consent and IRB approval before recording begins.

For a non-technical panellist

We used public datasets whose authors got the consent they needed. We did not record anyone ourselves. When we start the Rwandan recording phase, we will get formal ethics approval first.

Aren't you appropriating Deaf culture by automating it? Ethics
Technical answer

Automation is not appropriation when the artefact is open, the beneficiaries are named, and the work is positioned as accessibility infrastructure rather than as a replacement for the Deaf community. Our Future Work explicitly centres a partnership with the Rwanda National Union of the Deaf, not a unilateral product launch. Critically, SignLearn does not claim to translate ASL fluently — it recognises 36 isolated classes, which the thesis names clearly as fingerspelling and digits, not full language.

For a non-technical panellist

This system does not replace interpreters or Deaf people. It removes the burden in moments where no human help is available — like a clinic at 2 a.m. The next step is building it together with the Deaf community, not for them.

Scope concerns

Why didn't you do Kinyarwanda Sign Language (RSL)? You're at the University of Rwanda. Scope
Technical answer

RSL is the highest-value local extension and is named in §6.4 as long-horizon work. It is gated on a community-recording partnership with the Rwanda National Union of the Deaf, not on the model architecture — the pipeline is locale-neutral. Skipping it now is intentional; doing it well requires the partnership we are now positioned to begin.

For a non-technical panellist

We could not build a Rwandan sign-language model without data from Rwandan signers — and that requires partnership and ethics approval that take time. We deliberately built the system in a way that drops in RSL data when it exists, instead of building something we'd have to throw away.

Is this even sign-language recognition? You only do fingerspelling and digits. Scope
Technical answer

The production model recognises a 36-class subset of ASL — the alphabet and digits — which are real ASL lexical items used daily for proper nouns, numbers, and emphasis. The word-level research extension on ASL-Citizen demonstrates the same pipeline scales to 47–97 lexical signs. We do not claim full sentence translation; we frame the scope honestly in §1.4.1 and §6.3.

For a non-technical panellist

You're right that we don't translate full sentences yet — and our thesis says so clearly. What we do today is the part of sign language used for names, numbers, and key words. The word version is in the research extension, and full sentences is what's next.

Why no mobile app? Scope
Technical answer

The architecture supports it: MediaPipe Tasks API runs on mobile Chrome and Safari, the ONNX model is 538 KB, and the Next.js client is responsive. The reason we present on a laptop is to keep the defense demo on a single-host setup with no network variability. Mobile packaging is named in §6.4 alongside fully in-browser ONNX.

For a non-technical panellist

The phone version is easy and is on our roadmap — we are demoing on a laptop today only because the network is more predictable that way.

Non-technical / impact

Who actually uses this? Impact
For everyone

Four named groups. (1) Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing individuals in moments without an interpreter. (2) Frontline staff in hospitals, schools, and government offices — they can serve Deaf users without escalation. (3) ASL learners — including hearing parents of Deaf children — who get instant feedback on handshape. (4) Researchers, who get an open audited baseline instead of yet another closed system.

Why does this matter for Rwanda specifically? Impact
For everyone

Roughly 50,000 Deaf or Hard-of-Hearing Rwandans share a national interpreter pool of fewer than a hundred certified professionals. The asymmetry excludes Deaf Rwandans from classrooms, hospitals, courts, and government services in moments where an interpreter cannot be scheduled. The architecture is locale-neutral, so once a Kinyarwanda Sign Language dataset is collected — the work named in §6.4 — the system extends without code changes.

Is this a research project, a startup, or a public service? Impact
For everyone

A public-good research artefact. The licence is permissive. We have no startup intention; the intended adopters are the Rwanda National Union of the Deaf, the Ministry of ICT, and the research community. If a commercial entity wants to take the work forward, the open release does not prevent that.

If a Deaf person can just type on a phone, why is this useful? Impact
For everyone

Typing presupposes that both parties share a written language, that the Deaf user has a free hand, and that the asymmetric burden of translation falls on them. In an emergency triage or a classroom question, those preconditions fail. The contribution of SignLearn is not replacing typing — it's removing the translation burden from the Deaf user, and letting hearing people without ASL training participate in real time.

What is the next concrete step if you got funding tomorrow? Impact
For everyone

A community-partnered recording project with the Rwanda National Union of the Deaf for approximately 25 conversational words — three to five native Rwandan signers per class, twenty-five takes per signer-class pair, over a three-month window. Based on the lift we observed from the WLASL-to-ASL-Citizen pivot, we project this lifts the word model into the 90 %+ range on a meeting-vocabulary scope — sufficient for a real pilot deployment in a Rwandan public-service setting.

7. Closing message

The one sentence to leave the panel with — for both technical and non-technical members.

SignLearn demonstrates that high-quality, real-time sign recognition is achievable on commodity hardware with a careful landmark-plus-sequence-model architecture, and the only remaining barrier to conversational deployment is community-partnered data — not model capability.
